Key Understandings:

1.
Particles can move from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration passively. However, the reverse process requires energy input.
2.
Diffusion, osmosis, and active transport in living systems operate at the cellular level, across the partially permeable cell surface membrane.
3.
Living organisms rely on diffusion, osmosis, and active transport to perform cellular processes.

1.
Define diffusion as the movement of particles from a region of their higher concentration to a region of their lower concentration, down a concentration gradient


(a) Diffusion



  • Diffusion is the net movement of particles from a region of their higher concentration to a region of their lower concentration, down a concentration gradient


(b) Kinetic Particle Theory



  • Particles of matter possess kinetic energy and move about randomly and constantly (Brownian motion)
  • This is caused by collisions with other particles in the gas or liquid
  • This constant random motion eventually cause all particles to become evenly distributed throughout a defined area where a state of equilibrium is reached, i.e. there is no net movement of particles from one area to another

2.
Discuss the factors that affect the rate of diffusion


(a) Concentration Gradient & Distance



  • Concentration gradient is the difference in concentration of a type of particle between two regions
  • The steeper the concentration gradient, the faster the rate of diffusion
  • The shorter the distance the particles have to travel, the faster the rate of diffusion





(b) Temperature



  • As temperature increases, the particles possess more kinetic energy, rate of diffusion increases


(c) State of Particles & Diffusion Medium



  • Particles are molecules, atoms and ions that make up matter
  • Gases diffuse fastest, followed by liquids, then solids
  • Gas particles have the highest average kinetic energy and greatest the distance between particles (no fixed shape and no fixed volume) which causes them to diffuse and reach equilibrium faster
  • Diffusion in solids is known as Kirkendall Effect
  • At the boundary between two solid metals, the two atoms diffuse when spaces open, resulting in the formation of alloys


(d) Size of Particles



  • Smaller and lighter particles diffuse faster than larger and heavier particles
  • Given two particles of the same size, the heavier (denser) particle possess less kinetic energy (due to gravity)


(e) Surface Area to Volume Ratio



  • The larger the surface area to volume ratio of a cell, the faster the rate of diffusion
  • As the length of a side of a cube increases, its volume increases faster than its surface area and thus surface area to volume ratio decreases

3.
Define osmosis as the passage of water molecules from a region of higher water potential to a region of lower water potential, through a partially permeable membrane


(a) Osmosis



  • Osmosis is the net movement of water molecules from a solution of higher water potential to a solution of lower water potential, across a partially permeable membrane
    • Water potential is a measure of the tendency of water molecules to move from one area to another
    • A dilute solution has a higher water potential than a concentrated solution.

4.
Describe the importance of the cell surface membrane, water potential gradient, and the cell wall in the uptake of water by plants, and the effects of osmosis on plant and animal tissues


(a) Osmosis in cells



  • Osmosis can occur in cells because
    • It is surrounded by a partially permeable membrane
    • The cytoplasm and extracellular fluids mostly consist of water (with dissolved substances)


(b) Water Potential Gradient



  • A water potential gradient is created when a partially permeable membrane separates two solutions of different water potentials
  • A plant cell will behave differently from an animal cell due to the presence of a cell wall


(c) Osmosis in Animal Cells



  • When placed in a solution of higher water potential,
    • Water molecules enter the cell, causing the cell to swell and eventually bursts as it does not have a cell wall to protect it
    • If a red blood cell swells and bursts/lyses, it is called haemolysis
  • When placed in a solution of lower water potential,
    • Water molecules leave the cell, cuasing the cell to shrinks
    • In red blood cells, crenation occurs when little spikes appear on the cell surface membrane
    • If the cell is crenated for too long, the red blood cell will become dehydrated and die


(d) Osmosis in Plant Cells



  • When placed in a solution of higher water potential,
    • Water molecules enter the cell, causing the central vacuole to increase in size & push the cytoplasm against the cell wall
    • Cell enlarges and becomes turgid
    • The cell is in a state of turgor
      • The pressure exerted by the cell contents on the cell wall is the turgor pressure
    • The cell wall is strong and relatively inelastic, preventing overexpansion of the cell by exerting an opposing pressure as water molecules enter the cell, preventing the entry of more water molecules and preventing the cell from bursting
  • When placed in a solution of lower water potential,
    • As water molecules leave the cell, the central vacuole decreases in size, becoming flaccid
    • As water molecules continue to leave the cell, plasmolysis occurs, the cytoplasm and cell surface membrane to pull away from the cell wall, becoming plasmolysed
    • A plasmolysed cell can be restored to normal by placing it in a solution with higher water potential
    • However, if a cell is plasmolysed for too long, it will die
    • The space between the cell wall and plasma membrane contains the solution that the plant cell was placed in
  • Turgor pressure allows non-woody plant parts, e.g. leaves and young stems, to maintain their shape and upright positions
  • When plant cells lose water through osmosis, turgidity decreases, and cells become flaccid, and the plant begins to wilt
  • The opening and closing of stomata is controlled by changes in turgidity of the guard cells

5.
Define active transport as the movement of particles into or out of a cell through the cell surface membrane, from a region of their lower concentration to a region of their higher concentration against a concentration gradient, using energy released during respiration


(a) Active Transport



  • Active transport is the movement of particles from a region of lower concentration to a region of higher concentration, across the cell surface membrane, using energy released during respiration
  • Since active transport requires energy, it occurs only in respiring living cells
  • Active transport requires proteins (embedded in the cell surface membrane) to transport substances
  • Active transport allows cells to absorb substances even when these substances are at a higher concentration inside the cell

6.
Discuss how diffusion limits the size of cells and how plants and mammals are adapted for gaseous exchange and the importance of diffusion and active transport in ion uptake by root hairs and digested food molecule uptake in the villi cells


(a) Limitations on Cell Size



  • Larger cells require more nutrients and produce more waste products
  • However, there is a loss in surface area as volume increases, causing diffusion efficiency to be reduced
  • As it is slower for nutrients to reach the centre of a large cell to remove waste products, it would not be able to sustain itself
  • Thus, large multicellular organisms have specialised organs and cells to increase surface area to volume ratio for diffusion of nutrients in and waste products out


(b) Gaseous Exchange & Excretion in Animals



  • Lungs
    • Specialised organ for gaseous exchange in mammals
    • The lungs have a very large surface area because they contain millions of sacs called alveoli which are folded
    • The walls of the alveoli are only one cell thick, which shortens the diffusion distance for gaseous exchange to occur
    • The oxygen is transported by red blood cells via the blood vessels to cells in other parts of the body, that are not exposed to the outside environment
  • Kidneys
    • Specialised organ that filters blood to produce urine (which contains the waste products for excretion out of the body)
    • The kidneys have a very large surface area because the cells that make up the nephrons (tubules where urine is produced) have membranes that are folded


(c) Gaseous Exchange & Excretion in Plants



  • Leaves
    • Specialised organ for photosynthesis, gaseous exchange and excretion in plants
    • Diffusion of carbon dioxide in and oxygen out occur through the stomata of the leaves
    • Carbon dioxide dissolves into the film of water at the surface of the mesophyll cells before it diffuses into the cell where it is used for photosynthesis
    • The leaves are thin which shortens the diffusion distance diffusion of gases to occur
    • Some plants get rid of waste products by storing them within the leaf cell vacuoles before shedding them


(d) Importance of Diffusion & Active Transport



  • Red blood cells are biconcave in shape which increases surface area to volume ratio for more efficient diffusion of gases
  • Root hair cells contain many mitochondria and are elongated, which increases surface area to volume ratio for more efficient diffusion and active transport of mineral salts
  • Villi cells contain many mitochondria and are highly folded, which increases surface area to volume ratio for more efficient diffusion and active transport of digested food molecules
